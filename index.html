Why Your Employee Costs More Than Their Salary

Most small business owners think hiring a $60,000/year employee costs $60,000 a year. The real number is closer to $78,000–$84,000. The gap comes from mandatory employer taxes, insurance requirements, and benefits that rarely appear on job postings — but hit your bank account every month.

This tool adds up every cost the government requires you to pay on top of salary, so you can make an honest comparison before you sign an offer letter or a contractor agreement.

For 1099 contractors, you pay none of these on top — the contractor handles their own self-employment taxes and benefits. The trade-off is contractors often charge higher hourly rates to compensate. This calculator shows you the true apples-to-apples comparison.

A W2 employee typically costs 25%–40% more than their base salary. For a $60,000/year employee, the true annual cost including payroll taxes, unemployment insurance, workers compensation, and a basic benefits package is often $75,000–$85,000. This calculator breaks down every line item so you can see exactly where the extra cost comes from.
Employers pay 6.2% for Social Security (on the first $168,600 of wages), 1.45% for Medicare (no wage cap), 6% for FUTA federal unemployment insurance (on the first $7,000), and a state unemployment insurance rate (SUTA) that varies by state and typically ranges from 1%–5% for new employers. This calculator uses your selected state's standard new employer SUTA rate.
Yes — for the same take-home pay, a 1099 contractor costs significantly less because you don't owe payroll taxes, benefits, workers comp, or unemployment insurance. However, contractors typically charge higher rates to cover their own taxes and benefits. The key question is whether the contractor's rate plus project flexibility still saves you money compared to a fully-loaded employee. Use this calculator to compare the true numbers.
Misclassifying a W2 employee as a 1099 contractor is one of the most common — and costly — mistakes small businesses make. Penalties include back payroll taxes, interest, and IRS fines ranging from $50 to $1,000 per worker per violation, plus potential state penalties. The IRS uses a behavioral control, financial control, and relationship-type test to determine proper classification. When in doubt, consult a CPA or employment attorney before hiring.
Yes, significantly. Workers compensation rates are regulated by state and vary by industry risk classification (NCCI codes). An office worker might cost 0.3%–0.5% of wages, while a construction laborer can cost 10%–15% or more. Most states require workers comp coverage for all W2 employees from their first day of work. 1099 contractors are generally not covered under your policy — they must carry their own.
